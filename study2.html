Now, let's delve into the focal point of this case study – the cut-to-size online configurator. As the sole designer, I dedicated my efforts to investigate the current in-store offering. Collaborating with the research team, we devised a comprehensive user testing plan aimed at uncovering in-store pain points and understanding customer expectations for a potential online service. Operating within a dynamic agile environment with 4-week sprints, I closely collaborated with the Product Owner, research team, developers, the broader B&Q design team (including the team involved in the PDP), and other relevant teams integral to the facets this configurator would impact.

Problem Statement

Currently, there is only one route for a customer to get cut to-size timber, the in-store one.

There was a desire to investigate if offering an online alternative to order pre-cut products could increase the number of customers using the service and ultimately bring more revenue.

Design Process

User Research & Analysis

Before progressing on to other forms of design. I wanted to gain insight into validating the viability of providing an online cutting service. This was done with the hypothesis that customers would want to pay for the convenience of this service.

Investigation about the current in-store cut to size experience

Please find below the type of questions we made with its answers

What considerations are there when deciding to use the instore service? [Logistics, decision-making, etc]

  • Saving time and accurate result. To cut timber at the store is faster and more convenient than to do it by themselves;
  • No need to clean up after cutting;
  • Absence of necessary tools.
  • Distance to the store and delivery options.

Do users face any issues with in-store services?

  • 2/4 participants didn’t mention any critical issue with the in-store service except availability (sometimes it was closed);
  • 3/4 participants mentioned that they needed to wait in the line, and it was annoying.

Do they have any worries or concerns at any stage?

  • The main concern for all participants is to receive their pieces with correct measurements because sometimes even mm matters;
  • 1/4 participants mentioned that the first few times he had difficulties was when he didn’t draw the result with dimensions, what made the whole process harder for him and for the machine operator in the store.

What does success for the user look like? What do users consider an overall efficient in-store service to look like?

  • Ability to receive the cuts of correct size and shape/form;
  • Ability to quickly and efficiently communicate what they need;
  • Availability of seeing the timber needed next to the service with ability to choose what they need;
  • Quick delivery to home/workshop.

Do users expect self-service or guided assistance?

  • All participants expected guided assistance

Investigation about an online cut-to-size service

Please find below the type of questions we made with its answers

What considerations might there be when deciding to use the online service? [Logistics, etc]

  • Saving time and no need to wait in the line;
  • Ability to choose the timber from a list without having to bring it to the trolley and then to the till;
  • To be sure the measurements are good;
  • Home delivery;
  • Ability to see the result on a preview and play with measurements.

What time expectations do users have?

  • 1/4 participants noticed that it’s crucial for him to receive the items same day;
  • Ability to choose the timber from a list without having to bring it to the trolley and then to the till;
  • 3/4 participants mentioned that it would be great to receive the items same day,
  • however, next day delivery would also be fine for them.

Research highlights

Target users: Anyone who want to do a DIY project and need some cut to size timber.

  • Convenience: user doesn't need to have the right tools, no need to clean up the space after cutting
  • Time saving: Avoid in store queues or in store lack of service availability (the saw operator might be on lunch break)
  • Efficiency: Ability to quickly and effectively communicate what they want (no need to bring the measurements written on paper or in any device to the store) Ability to choose the timber from a list without having to bring it to the trolley and then go to the till

User Stories

As a DIY customer seeking to buy timber, I would like to search for the correct timber for my purpose and to calculate the correct quantity and sizes. So I can be assured my timber purchase will meet my functional, aesthetic and budget objectives.

Iterative Process

As evident from the attached screenshots, following my research and gaining a clearer understanding of user goals, I initiated the creation of wireframes. Preceding this, a comprehensive analysis of competitors was undertaken. It became imperative to harmonise the UX strategy with the overarching business goals and objectives, ensuring that the user experience becomes a pivotal contributor to the company's success. This led to an in-depth investigation into the functionality of the current Product Listing Page (PLP) and Product Detail Page (PDP), with a focus on how to seamlessly introduce and promote the new cut-to-size service at the PLP level. Additionally, efforts were directed toward ensuring alignment with the existing design system and strategising the integration of the cut-to-size configurator within the current PDP page.

I commenced the process with mobile-first wireframes, exploring a bespoke cut-to-size configurator featuring a 3D preview. This would allow users to visualise the cuts and quickly discern situations where additions were not feasible due to a lack of timber surface.

Following discussions with developers, we determined that a bespoke cut-to-size configurator with a 3D preview was too complex to develop. The algorithm required to detect the size of the timber and evenly distribute bespoke sizes provided by the customer posed challenges. Consequently, I iterated towards a solution featuring even cuts, based on research indicating that most customers preferred halves or multiples of the same size. This option appeared more feasible for both user utility and development practicality.

  1. The chosen option was a mixed tab menu, enabling customers to choose between preset cuts or a specific number of equal cuts
  2. For collect in store option, additional considerations that I had to address involved the fact that this service was tailored to function within a specific barcode system, namely EAN (European Article Number). Whether a customer is logged in or not, their access to the service remains unaffected. However, the location a customer designates as preferred during registration will impact its functionality, considering that not all stores may provide cut-to-size services (availability varied among stores).
  3. To navigate this nuance, potential solutions were explored. Options included delivering the item from another store with the offering, or prompting the user to choose an alternative nearby store from an available list. Strategies were devised to effectively manage this aspect of the service.

Final Thoughts

Unfortunately, this project was not built due to an excessive number of dependencies and Kingfisher's ongoing technology transformation, and the project relied heavily on that. It is possible that this project may be revisited in the future. If that happens, there are expectations to conduct further user testing on the proposed versions in order to be sure the expectations of customers are meet for a cut-to-size online service.
